Introduction to Organic Chemistry

Understanding what organic compounds are, their origin, and the historical foundation of the discipline.

Organic Chemistry is the branch of chemistry concerned with the study of organic compounds — substances composed of carbon (C) and hydrogen (H), along with their derivatives. The word "organic" originally referred to substances derived from living organisms.
What is an Organic Compound?

Compounds formed primarily from carbon (C) and hydrogen (H) are called hydrocarbons. All hydrocarbons and their derived groups — where other elements (O, N, Cl, etc.) replace hydrogen — are collectively called organic compounds.

Historical Background
⚗️
Vitalist Theory — Berzelius (1807)

Jöns Jacob Berzelius proposed that organic compounds could only be produced by living organisms through a mysterious "vital force." This theory dominated early chemistry.

🔬
Wöhler's Synthesis — Friedrich Wöhler (1828)

Wöhler disproved the vitalist theory by synthesising urea (an organic compound) from the inorganic salt ammonium cyanate in a laboratory, proving organic compounds can be made artificially.

Wöhler's Reaction
NH₄Cl + Pb(CNO)₂ ⟶ NH₄CNO + PbCl₂
NH₄CNO Δ (heat) ⟶ NH₂–CO–NH₂ (Urea — first synthetic organic compound)
🧪 Quick Check: What did Wöhler's 1828 experiment prove?
✓ Wöhler made urea (NH₂CONH₂) from NH₄CNO, disproving the vitalist theory and founding modern organic chemistry.

Classification of Organic Compounds

How organic compounds are organised based on their carbon skeleton and degree of saturation.

Organic compounds are classified by the shape of their carbon skeleton (open-chain or ring) and by the types of bonds they contain (single, double, or triple).
1. Acyclic / Open-chain Compounds (Aliphatic)

Carbon atoms are joined in a straight or branched chain — no ring. Called "aliphatic" from the Greek word for fat (they were first extracted from fats).

2. Cyclic / Ring Compounds

Carbon atoms form a closed ring. Divided into carbocyclic (all C in ring) and heterocyclic (other atoms in ring).

IUPAC Naming Rules for Alkanes
Rule 1: Find the longest carbon chain +
Identify the longest continuous chain of carbon atoms. This gives the parent alkane name. Example: a chain of 6 carbons = hexane parent.
Rule 2: Number the chain from the nearest branch +
Number the carbons starting from the end nearer to the first branch point. The substituent should get the lowest possible number.
Rule 3: Name and number the substituents +
Each branch is named as an alkyl group with its position number as a prefix. Multiple identical groups use prefixes: di-, tri-, tetra-.
Rule 4: Alphabetical order for substituents +
When there are multiple different substituents, list them alphabetically (ignoring di/tri prefixes). E.g.: 3-ethyl-2-methylpentane.

Carboxylic Acids

Organic acids with the –COOH (carboxyl) functional group. Suffix: -oic acid. General formula: CₙH₂ₙO₂.

Carboxylic acids contain the –COOH (carboxyl) group = a carbonyl (C=O) and a hydroxyl (–OH) on the same carbon. General formula: H/R–COOH. Named by replacing terminal -e of alkane with -oic acid.
IUPAC Naming Rules
Rule 1: Choose the longest chain including –COOH +
The carboxyl carbon MUST be included in the parent chain. It always gets position number 1 (C-1). So for HCOOH: 1 carbon chain = methanoic acid.
Rule 2: Number from the carboxyl carbon +
The –COOH carbon is always C-1. Number the rest of the chain from there.
Rule 3: Name branches with position numbers +
Add substituent names with position numbers before the acid name. Example: CH₃CH(CH₃)CH₂COOH = 3-methylbutanoic acid.

Isomerism — Cis-Trans & Dipole Moment

Geometric isomers arise from restricted rotation about a C=C double bond. Cis and trans isomers differ in physical properties including melting point and dipole moment.

Geometric isomerism (cis-trans) occurs when rotation around a double bond is restricted, and the same groups can be on the same side (cis) or opposite sides (trans) of the double bond.
Conditions for Cis-Trans Isomerism
Condition 1: Restricted rotation (C=C or ring) +
Free rotation around C=C is NOT possible (unlike C–C). This means groups attached to the double bond are locked in place, giving rise to different spatial arrangements.
Condition 2: Two DIFFERENT groups on EACH double bond carbon +
If one double bond carbon has two identical groups (e.g., CH₂=), no cis/trans isomerism is possible. Each C in the C=C must have two different substituents.
Cis vs Trans — But-2-ene
Cis-but-2-ene
Both CH₃ groups are on the same side of the double bond.
B.P.: 3.7°C  |  Dipole moment: μ ≠ 0
Trans-but-2-ene
CH₃ groups are on opposite sides of the double bond.
B.P.: 0.9°C  |  Dipole moment: μ = 0
Dipole Moment (μ)
Dipole moment (μ) is a measure of polarity. A bond between two different atoms has a dipole. When bond dipoles add up (cis isomers), μ ≠ 0. When they cancel out (trans isomers), μ = 0.
CH₂Cl₂ (dichloromethane): H–C(δ+)–Cl(δ−) → μ = 1.91 D (Debye) The two C–Cl bond dipoles point same direction → net dipole ≠ 0 CHCl₃ (symmetrical arrangement): Four identical bonds cancel out → μ = 0 Key rule from notes: → Cis isomers: dipole moments ADD → higher μ → Trans isomers: dipole moments CANCEL → μ = 0 (or very small)

🧪 Quick Check: Why does the trans isomer of 2-butene have a dipole moment of zero?
✓ In trans-2-butene, the two C–CH₃ bond dipoles point in exactly opposite directions and cancel. The net dipole moment = 0. In the cis form, the dipoles point in similar directions and add up.
